# Princeton GEO Metrics — The Math

Source: Aggarwal, P., Murahari, V., Rajpurohit, T., Kalyan, A., Narasimhan, K., & Deshpande, A. (2024). *GEO: Generative Engine Optimization*. ACM SIGKDD 2024. [arXiv:2311.09735](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.09735)

This is the only peer-reviewed academic foundation for AI-visibility measurement that exists at the time of writing. Every vendor metric — Profound's Share of Voice, AthenaHQ's Brand Mention Rate, HubSpot AEO's Brand Visibility Score, Semrush's AI Share of Voice — is some flavor of these three metrics, usually without disclosed formulas. We use the Princeton definitions directly so our numbers are reproducible and comparable to the literature.

## Notation

For a single AI response `R` generated by engine `E` for prompt `Q`:
- `R` consists of sentences `s_1, s_2, ..., s_n`
- Each sentence may have one or more citations `c_1, c_2, ...`
- Each citation `c` points to a source URL `u`
- For a source URL `u`, the **eligible content** is the set of sentences from `u` that were retrieved into the engine's context window for this query (proxied by what `u` could plausibly answer based on its on-page content)

We track these per (page × prompt × engine × language × date × sample_index).

## Metric 1: Impression Score (Position-Adjusted Word Count)

**Definition.** The fraction of the response that consists of content attributed to the target source, weighted by position in the response.

**Formula.**

```
ImpressionScore(u, R) = Σ_{i: cite(s_i) = u} (word_count(s_i) × position_weight(i))
                       ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
                                  Σ_{j=1..n} word_count(s_j)
```

Where:
- `cite(s_i) = u` means sentence `s_i` is attributed to source `u`
- `position_weight(i)` is a monotonically decreasing function of position. Aggarwal et al. use the inverse-rank weighting: `position_weight(i) = 1 / log2(i + 1)` (the DCG kernel).

**Range:** [0, 1]. Higher is better. A score of 0.3 means roughly 30% of the response (position-weighted) came from your source.

**Intuition.** A citation in the first sentence of a 5-sentence answer is worth far more than a citation in the last sentence of a 30-sentence answer. This metric captures that.

**Reference implementation.** See `scripts/princeton_score.py::impression_score`.

## Metric 2: Citation Recall

**Definition.** The fraction of the eligible content from your source that actually appears in the response.

**Formula.**

```
CitationRecall(u, R, Q) = |{sentences in u that informed sentences in R}|
                          ───────────────────────────────────────────────
                          |{sentences in u that could have informed R for Q}|
```

**Operationalization.** Since we don't have access to the engine's retrieval set, we proxy the denominator by:
1. Running a local BM25 + sentence-transformer retrieval over `u`'s content with `Q` as the query
2. Taking the top-K sentences (K=5 by default) as the "could have informed" set
3. Matching numerator sentences against this set via cosine similarity > 0.7

This is approximate, but it's the same proxy Aggarwal et al. use and it's stable for trend tracking.

**Range:** [0, 1]. Higher is better.

**Use case.** Diagnoses *content gaps*. Low recall means the engine didn't surface the part of your page that's relevant — usually because the relevant content is buried below the fold or behind JavaScript (the SPA problem).

## Metric 3: Citation Precision

**Definition.** The fraction of citations to your source that are factually accurate — your brand named correctly, attributes not hallucinated, claims faithful to your source page.

**Formula.**

```
CitationPrecision(u, R) = |{accurate citations to u in R}|
                          ───────────────────────────────
                          |{total citations to u in R}|
```

**Operationalization.** For each citation `c_i` pointing to `u`:
1. Extract the claim attributed to `u` in `R`
2. LLM-judge (Claude Opus 4.6 by default, see `references/engine_specs.md`) determines if the claim is supported by `u`'s actual content with a faithfulness rubric (0 = contradicts, 1 = partial, 2 = fully supported)
3. Count only score=2 as accurate

**Range:** [0, 1]. Higher is better.

**Use case.** Diagnoses *hallucination risk*. If your Citation Precision drops, the engine is making things up about you. This is a brand-safety signal as much as an SEO one.

## Sampling and confidence intervals

**Critical.** AI responses are stochastic. A single observation is a sample from a distribution, not a measurement. Per IQRush (2026), citation share can vary by ±15-30% across runs of identical queries.

**Protocol.** For each (page × prompt × engine × language × date):
1. Sample N=5 responses (default; configurable per engine — see `references/engine_specs.md`)
2. Compute each metric for each sample
3. Report `mean ± 95% CI` using bootstrap (10,000 resamples)
4. Store every raw response in `aeo_response_observations` table — never throw away samples

**Reporting rule.** Any number that appears in a digest, dashboard, or PR description must be in the form `value ± CI`. Bare point estimates are forbidden.

## Aggregating across aeo_prompts: Share of Answer

For competitive benchmarking, we aggregate per source:

```
ShareOfAnswer(u, prompt_set, E) = mean over q ∈ prompt_set of ImpressionScore(u, R_q^E)
```

This is what we report against aeo_citation_pools. Critically:
- It's computed per engine. ChatGPT and Perplexity have very different citation behaviors; reporting a "global" Share of Answer is misleading.
- It's computed per prompt set. The 50 buyer-intent aeo_prompts are the primary KPI; the 50 top-of-funnel aeo_prompts are a secondary signal.
- It includes the CI. `cinecanon.com: 23.4% ± 2.1%` is a legitimate report; `cinecanon.com: 23%` is not.

## What "improvement" means

A metric improvement is real if:
1. The mean moved in the right direction, AND
2. The 95% CIs of the before and after distributions don't overlap, OR
3. A paired test (we use bootstrap difference-of-means, 10k resamples) shows p < 0.05

If the CIs overlap and p > 0.05, the move is noise, not improvement. The content-optimizer agent will not claim credit for a noise move, and won't merge a PR based on one.

## Why these three are sufficient

Aggarwal et al. show that these three metrics jointly capture the full visibility surface:
- **Impression Score** captures *how much of the answer is you*
- **Citation Recall** captures *how much of you the answer used*
- **Citation Precision** captures *whether it got you right*

Most vendor metrics conflate the first and third (Share of Voice, Brand Mention Rate) and ignore the second. Recall is what tells you *why* you have low impression — and it's the metric that maps directly to fixable on-page issues (the SPA problem, content burial, schema absence).

## The empirical effect-size table

From Aggarwal et al. (KDD 2024) — Table 2, 10,000 queries, GEO-bench:

| Intervention | ΔImpression Score | ΔSubjective Impression | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|
| Statistics Addition | **+41%** | +37% | **APPLY** |
| Cite Sources | **+40%** | +35% | **APPLY** |
| Cite Sources (rank 5+) | **+115%** | +98% | **APPLY (high lift)** |
| Quotation Addition | **+28%** | +26% | **APPLY** |
| Authoritative Language | +5% | +7% | Neutral |
| Easy-to-Understand | +3% | +4% | Neutral |
| Technical Terms | +2% | +1% | Neutral |
| Fluency Optimization | -2% | -3% | Neutral |
| Keyword Stuffing | **-10%** | -8% | **FORBIDDEN** |
| Unique Words | -4% | -5% | Negative |

The `content-optimizer` agent applies only the four "APPLY" interventions. It is explicitly forbidden from applying keyword stuffing even if asked. See `references/geo_interventions.md` for the operational details.

## 2026 updates not in the original paper

These are findings from post-Princeton work that we treat as supplementary evidence:

- **Page length:** pages >20,000 characters average 10.18 citations; <500 chars average 2.39. (Growth Memo, March 2026, n=12,500 queries)
- **Heading hierarchy:** 68.7% of ChatGPT citations come from pages with clean H1→H2→H3 nesting. (Foundation Marketing, March 2026)
- **Freshness:** content updated within 30 days has a 3.2x citation multiplier. (Semrush, ConvertMate cross-analysis, 2026)
- **Brand mentions vs backlinks:** brand mentions correlate 3x more strongly with AI citation than backlinks. (Ahrefs 75K-domain study, 2025)
- **Own-domain ceiling:** ~15% of AI citations come from own domain regardless of content investment. (LoudFace audit, 400+ B2B answers, 2026)

The last point is the strongest argument for `earned-media-hunter`. No amount of on-domain optimization breaks the 15% ceiling.
