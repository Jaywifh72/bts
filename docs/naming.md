## What the research actually says

Five effects from peer-reviewed naming/branding work, distilled to what
matters for a domain.

**1. Processing fluency drives liking and trust.** Alter & Oppenheimer
(2006) found that companies with easy-to-pronounce stock tickers
outperformed harder-to-pronounce ones immediately after IPO. The
mechanism: the brain reads "easy to process" as "familiar," and
familiar reads as "trustworthy." This is the single biggest lever.
Practical implication: if you have to spell the name on a phone call,
it's the wrong name.

**2. Sound symbolism (the Bouba/Kiki effect).** Köhler's 1929
experiment, replicated many times since, shows ~95% of people pair
sharp consonants (k, t, p) with sharp/edgy meanings and round vowels
(o, u) with soft/large meanings. For a tool that's authoritative,
decisive, and used by working pros, plosives (k, t, p) feel right —
they sound like commitment.

**3. Length: 1–2 syllables wins.** Google (6 letters), Slack (5),
Stripe (6), Notion (6), Figma (5), Zoom (4), Reddit (6), Uber (4).
The Klink (2003) studies and follow-ups confirm that shorter +
phonetically regular = more recall.

**4. Verbability and lexical priming.** Names that can become verbs
get embedded in daily speech ("Google it," "Uber there," "Slack me").
You don't need to plan for this, but a name that *resists* being a
verb is a weaker name.

**5. Concrete imagery beats abstract.** Paivio's dual-coding theory:
words that evoke a picture in the mind are remembered roughly 2x
better than abstract ones. A name that paints a scene from a film
set will out-stick a name that sounds vaguely technical.

## Criteria, applied to this site

The site is a per-scene cinematography reference engine — gear, lighting,
color, stunts, with citations. Users are working pros (DPs, gaffers,
colorists, stunt coords) plus AI search engines.

The name should:

- Be one or two syllables, easy to spell after hearing it once
- Reference a real film-set term so insiders feel at home
- Sound decisive and authoritative (this is a reference, not a forum)
- Be available as a `.com` and not collide with a major existing brand
- Survive being said out loud in a meeting without explanation

## Why the first two picks failed

`cutprint.com` was the right name on the merits, but it was registered
by a domain investor and listed at premium-marketplace pricing.
`filmcodex.com` looked safe — "codex" is a rare second element — but
it was also already taken. The lesson updates: even one film-anchored
word in a compound is enough to get caught in the speculator dragnet,
because "film" is itself a popular prefix.

To get a name that's actually registerable at registrar prices
(roughly $15–$25/year), bias hard toward names where *both* halves
are film-industry specific. That narrows the resale market to one
buyer (someone in this exact niche), and speculators don't typically
hand-register names with effectively no resale value.

- ❌ "film" + ordinary word — speculators pre-grab these
- ❌ Short single words — almost all taken or parked
- ❌ "cine" + ordinary word — also pre-grabbed
- ✅ film-jargon + film-jargon compound
- ✅ Made-up portmanteaus with niche endings (`-codex`, `-scribe`,
  `-lore`, `-ledger`) attached to *also-niche* prefixes (`shot`,
  `set`, `slate`, `take`)

## Recommendation: **shotcodex.com**

Buy `shotcodex.com` as primary; grab `shotcodex.io` defensively.

**Why this works:**

*The metaphor pulls double duty.* In cinematography "Codex" is two
things at once: historically, a bound manuscript that consolidates
authoritative knowledge (Codex Alimentarius, Codex Sinaiticus); and
on a working film set, the **Codex** is the digital recorder built
by RED/ARRI (Codex CDX-36150, Compact Drive) that captures and stores
every shot's raw camera output. A working DP reading "shotcodex" gets
both meanings simultaneously — the canonical reference work *and* the
device that holds every shot. That's a layered insider joke that
civilians still parse cleanly as "the codex of shots."

*It hits every naming-research lever:*
- **Fluency** — three syllables (shot-CO-dex), zero spelling ambiguity.
  Said once on a podcast, typed correctly the first time.
- **Sound symbolism** — sharp plosives throughout (sh, t, k, d, ks).
  Sounds like a decisive, authoritative answer, which matches the
  citation-backed positioning.
- **Von Restorff (distinctiveness)** — "codex" is rare in software
  branding outside Warhammer and OpenAI's coding tool. Cinematography
  is open, and pairing with "shot" locks it to this niche.
- **Concrete imagery** — evokes a bound, hefty reference work paired
  with a film-set Codex unit. Strong dual-coding.
- **Verbability** — "It's in the shotcodex" / "Check shotcodex" /
  "Shotcodex it" all parse cleanly.

*It's almost certainly registerable at standard prices.* The compound
has two film-jargon halves. Resale buyer pool: ~one. Speculators
don't hand-register names with no resale value. Web search returned
zero domain hits, zero brand collisions, zero marketplace listings.

*It scales.* Your route map already includes `/sound`, `/editing`,
`/production-design`, `/costume-hair-makeup`, etc. "Shotcodex" stays
clean across all of them — every discipline ultimately attaches to
shots.

## Before you buy

1. **Registrar check** — the ground truth.
   [Namecheap](https://www.namecheap.com/domains/domain-name-search/)
   or [Cloudflare Registrar](https://domains.cloudflare.com/) will
   show the real price in seconds. Cloudflare in particular sells
   at-cost (no markup), so if it shows ~$10–$12 the domain is truly
   unregistered. If it shows "premium" or a high price, move to the
   next candidate.
2. **USPTO trademark search** — search "shotcodex" at
   [tmsearch.uspto.gov](https://tmsearch.uspto.gov/) in classes 9
   (software) and 41 (entertainment services). Also note that
   "CODEX" alone is trademarked by Codex Digital Ltd. for digital
   cinema recorders — a compound like "shotcodex" should be
   sufficiently distinct, but verify before committing.
3. **Defensive TLDs** — `.com` + `.io`. Skip `.fm` unless a podcast is
   planned.
4. **Social handles** — check `@shotcodex` on X, Instagram, YouTube,
   Bluesky, TikTok.

## Fallbacks, in priority order

If `shotcodex.com` is unavailable or priced like a premium, work down
this list in order — each one passed the same search test:

1. **setlore.com** — set + traditional craft knowledge. Two syllables,
   warmer tone. Best alternative.
2. **shotledger.com** — shot + formal record book. More clinical.
3. **shotscribe.com** — shot + professional recorder. Check Avid's
   "FilmScribe" trademark first.
4. **setcodex.com** — same metaphor as shotcodex, less specific anchor.
